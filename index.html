🧂 #needsmoresalt

Taste your AI before you serve it.

Workslop problems?

Everyone's using generative AI now. Most of the time that's cool, and we shouldn't feel bad about it.

But unreviewed AI output (slop!) creates work for other people. You've seen it in tickets, Slack threads, emails, documentation, code comments, etc. Nobody reviewed it before hitting send, and it shows. It's confident about things it shouldn't be. It answers questions nobody asked. It throws a thousand ideas on the wall that all seem like you might need to consider, until you roll up your sleeves and take a close look. Ugh.

Would you cook for your friends like that?

How hot is this chili crisp? I guess we'll see. Enough lemon? Who knows! Too much garlic? No such thing! Needs salt? If only there was some way to find out in advance...


You probably wouldn't cook like this. We taste as we go, and tweak and adjust till we get it right.


AI is a powerful tool that can help us solve problems that are just a little out of reach, automate things that are tedious, and improve the quality of our work. But we can't just hand it off to each other. Read what it wrote. Check whether it's right. Make sure it says what you mean. Put your name on it only when you'd stand behind it and happily chat about it.

How it works.

When you spot AI output that clearly hasn't been reviewed, and you're debating whether you have the energy to dive in and try to make sense of it, and feeling stressed or resentful, and wondering if its OK to push back, reply:

#needsmoresalt

It's not a callout, it's a friendly reminder that we are all responsible for making sense.

As authors, we take another pass, make sure we signal what we stand behind, what we aren't sure about, what's speculative, what's a strong view, etc.

The recipe

  1. Use AI to generate something. Great.
  2. Read it. Revise it. Make it yours.
  3. Post it. Be prepared to answer questions about it.
  4. If someone says #needsmoresalt, take another pass. No hard feelings.

The norms.

This only works if the social contract goes both ways.

01

Nobody feels bad about using AI. We all feel weird about it at times, and they didn't actually ask us before they dropped it on us, but for many of us, in the work we do, this is our life now.

02

Nobody feels bad about flagging workslop. You're helping.

03

Nobody feels bad when asked to revise. It's like someone found a bug in your code. Or they asked for a snack and you sent a turkey dinner by accident. Just check it over.

04

It goes in every direction. I've passed on slop that I should have been called out on. I felt genuinely bad that I wasted someone's time with it.

Why I made this

I find I'm increasingly working with people and AI and we need a way to navigate the tendency of genAI to overwhelm us.

I don't mean to:
  • Shame people for using AI
  • Police how people write
  • Play gotcha
What I hope for is:
  • A nudge to take accountability for what we publish
  • Trying to bootstrap a friendly but useful epistemic norm, not a rule
  • A non-threatening and low-friction feedback mechanism

Think of it like linking a Stack Overflow answer and writing "I dunno, maybe this?", that's totally fine. But it's different from presenting that answer as your own considered take. #needsmoresalt is a friendly way to say: this reads like the first thing, and we need the second thing.

If you like this, pass it on.

You can link to this as a gentle way to introduce this norm in places you work.
